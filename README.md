# IITD MTech CSE - the unfiltered version

*for those considering IIT Delhi PG - before you make the call*

## 0. eligibility and how admissions work

before anything else - make sure you actually qualify.

**eligibility for M.Tech CSE (full-time):**

- BTech / BE in any discipline, OR
- BS (4 years) in CS / Math from IISc or IISER, OR
- Bachelor + Master or integrated master's (total at least 5 years) in Math / Statistics / Computer Science, OR
- MCA

you also need to show familiarity with Data Structures, Discrete Mathematics, and Digital Logic / Computer Organization through courses in your undergraduate or master's education. if these weren't in your curriculum, your application can get terminated.

**how selection works:**

for the full-time M.Tech CSE program - **no interview, no written test.** selection is purely based on CS GATE score. being shortlisted does not mean you're selected - it just means you're in the pool. final call is the score.

the only exception: IIT BTechs with CGPA >= 8.0 are exempted from GATE and go through an online interview instead.

**HVA and part-time / sponsored programs** have an in-person written test followed by interviews. the written test covers: data structures and algorithms, hardware and software systems, probability and linear algebra, discrete mathematics and logic. there's a sample paper on the official admissions page - go through it if you're applying for HVA.

**one thing different from other IITs:**

**there is no order of preference system here.** unlike other IITs where you can rank programs, IIT Delhi requires you to fill a separate application for each program independently. so if you want to apply to both M.Tech CSE and M.Tech Ctech, that's two separate forms. keep this in mind and don't miss any program you want to be considered for.

if you feel you should have been shortlisted but aren't - double check the shortlisting criteria and your application. if it still seems wrong, email the M.Tech coordinator (details on the official admissions page). no phone calls.

## 1. what are your options here?

**2-year programs (structured, placement pipeline strong):**
- M.Tech CSE - the classic, most popular
- M.Tech Cyber Security
- M.Tech Ctech (computing technology, A BIT ELECTRICAL CS MIDDLEGROUND)
- M.Tech JTM 
- M.Tech Robotics

**3-YeaR programs:**
- M.Tech CSE - HVA [~22k stipend, interview-based selection]
- M.Tech Ctech HVA [~22k stipend]
- MSR CSE
- MSR SIT (systems and IT)
- MSR Bharti / JTM

**rough seat count:**
- ~60 seats for MCS
- ~25 for JCS
- ~20 for Ctech
- verify with admin before applying, these change

**which one should you pick?**

if placement is your primary goal - M.Tech CSE 2-year is the safest bet. most structured, best pipeline, largest senior network.

if you want research and a longer runway - MSR or HVA. higher stipend but more commitment. placement support is less structured.

Cyber and Ctech have similar placement opportunities but more program-specific core courses, so balancing everything is harder. know why you want them before picking.

## 2. placements - the real picture

**the numbers (2026 batch, approximate):**

roughly 24 students sat for placement. 3 were still in phase 2 after main season - 1 HVA and 2 CSE 2-year. placement rate is high but not 100%. phase 2 is doable but it's more effort and stress.

**package wise:**
- M.Tech CSE: average ~37-38L, median ~34L
- Ctech: ~20L
- Cyber: ~25-26L average

**companies from the 2026 batch (where people are actually joining):**
Postman, Scifin, Accenture, Systems, Barclays, Nvidia, Harness, SAP, Naavi, Oracle, ARM, Qualcomm, Thoughtspot, Samsung, EXL... the list is longer but these are the real ones, not just logos on a brochure.

**what the brochure won't tell you:**

placement here is not passive. the IIT tag helps but it doesn't carry you through interviews. for SDE roles you'll face full DSA rounds and some system design. the batches that do well are the ones that started prep early and stayed consistent.

there's some UG politics in OCS (the placement cell) and gatekeeping around who gets into the main body. exact stats never appear on official channels. talk to current students for ground truth.

**placement rules here are stricter than most other IITs.** there are policies around how many companies you can sit for, what happens after you accept an offer, dream offer rules etc. read them carefully before season starts. not knowing the rules has burned people before.

you generally need **>7.5 CG to sit for most companies comfortably.** some have hard cutoffs. keep this in mind from day one.

**the bottom line:**

IIT Delhi is genuinely one of the better options for PG placements in India. but it's a platform, not a guarantee. you still have to show up.

## 3. the new curriculum - things have changed

if you're reading older guides, some of this won't match.

**what changed:**
- bridge courses removed entirely (older guides talk about waiver exams - that's gone)
- total credit requirement: 57 credits
- structure: 6 core + 24 (MTP) + 6 (cornerstone + intern) + 21 (PE + OE)

**the MTP is not something you can delay:**

the major technical project is a big chunk of credits and needs to be treated seriously from day one. people who let it drift early end up in serious trouble later. it compounds. go to your supervisor regularly, don't wait for things to be "ready."

**the gap nobody warns you about:**

CN (computer networks) and OS are no longer formally offered to MTechs the way they used to be. companies will ask OS and networking questions regardless. if your undergrad was non-CS, or if you're rusty - you will need to self-study these on your own time.

**almost every course here assumes you know the basics.** this isn't a place that rebuilds fundamentals for you. if you're weak coming in, learning basics while keeping up with advanced coursework is genuinely brutal.

**the upside:**

more elective flexibility. systems, AI/ML, security - good courses exist if you choose deliberately.

## 4. the drop rate - something nobody talks about

**IIT Delhi's drop rate is among the highest of any IIT for PG CSE.**

the senior batch came in with 46 entries. by placement season, only 2 remained. for the 25-27 batch, there were 54 entries at start - current strength is close to 40 with drops already happening.

why do people drop - workload becomes unmanageable, CG falls and stipend goes, some get jobs mid-program, health and personal reasons get amplified by the pressure.

this isn't to scare you. plenty of people complete it fine. but know what the actual completion landscape looks like before deciding. if you join, commit seriously.

## 5. hostel situation - manage expectations

**the policy:**

PG students get hostel for only 2 semesters out of 4. it's typically not sem 1+2 - could be sem 2+3 or sem 3+4. sem 1 you will mostly be living off campus.

**exception: all female students always get hostel.** if you're female, this section is less relevant for you.

**for male students - off-campus reality:**

main areas: Jia Sarai, Katwaria Sarai, Ber Sarai. most are 10 minutes walking to LHC and the MCS lab. not glamorous but functional. hygiene is average, crowded, you will compromise.

**cost breakdown:**
- non-AC sharing: 5-7k/month
- AC sharing (2 people): 7-9k/month (personal bathroom may or may not be included)
- non-AC single: 9-13k/month
- AC single: 12-15k/month

food not included in most places - mess food is ~6k/month extra. electricity bill is separate too.

seniors share room links on WhatsApp groups. **do not pay anyone online without seeing the room first.** photos are always misleading and scam risk is real. if this repo gets enough traction, current students will share verified listings here - watch this space.

**the silver lining:**

you don't get hostel but you do get **lab space**. the MCS lab is accessible, AC, full facilities, and many students practically live there - some stay 2 days continuous during project crunch. it becomes your second home genuinely.

**on-campus when you get it:**

one sem non-AC, next AC. campus hostel life is good - mess, community, everything walkable.

**fees:**
- on-campus hostel: ~36k/semester (food + room bundled)
- tuition: ~24k/semester, first semester ~40k (admission fees on top)

## 6. stipend - who gets it

stipend is CG-dependent, not automatic.

**thresholds (2025-27 batch, 54 enrollments):**
- 10 students above 8 CG
- 3 students above 9 CG
- 17 above 7.5 CG
- ~25 above 7 CG

verify with official circular - these are ballpark.

CG below 7 = no stipend + financial pressure stacking on top of academic pressure. keep grades up.

on GATE scores - last year MCS dropped to 760 in spot round from 800 (GEN category). spot rounds at IIT Delhi are rare, mostly just one case in the last few years. if you get in via spot, the score stops mattering internally after that.

## 7. academics, faculty, and research

**the workload:**

from day one, you will not have free days. every day is one or more deadlines. no real summer or winter breaks - the academic calendar fills everything. coursework, TA duties, project work, and placement prep all happening simultaneously. sem 1 is the hardest adjustment for most people.

**TA work and attendance are serious:**

there is a strict daily attendance policy at the CSE office. TA work is not casual - you're expected to show up and be reliable. people have had real problems from not treating it properly.

**faculty - the honest picture:**

systems and AI/ML faculty here are genuinely top tier. a few names that come up consistently:
- Sarangi's COA/OS - excellent if systems is your thing
- Mausam's AI - highly regarded
- Parag's ML - solid for AI/ML track
- proof techniques sounds dry, is actually useful

that said - **some profs are egoistic and visibly biased towards BTechs.** the discrimination is real, it's just a few not the majority. you'll figure out who fast. don't let it derail you, but know it exists so you're not blindsided.

**plagiarism is taken very seriously.**

submissions go through checks and the consequences are not light. don't copy code, don't copy reports. it's not worth it.

**on research:**

real opportunity if you want it. do it for genuine interest, not resume points. if you're going MSR, you're committed - go all in.

## 8. the hidden curriculum - connections and information

**IIT Delhi runs on information and connections more than raw effort alone.**

which prof to take, which supervisor for MTP, which companies have easier rounds, which seniors to ask for referrals, what the actual placement rules mean in practice, which elective is genuinely useful - none of this is written anywhere official.

people who navigate this place well are the ones who talk to seniors, stay plugged into informal channels, and don't try to figure everything out alone.

join the WhatsApp groups, go to department events, talk to your seniors. it pays back more than you'd expect.

if you want to talk to current MCS students before deciding - reach out to the CSE department or raise an issue on this repo. students from the batch are willing to answer questions.

## 9. delhi itself

campus is south Delhi, good connectivity, green, plenty of food nearby. once settled it's a genuinely nice place.

but delhi comes with real downsides:

**pollution is serious** - winters especially. air quality hits dangerous levels for weeks. if you have any respiratory sensitivity, factor this in. it affects energy levels more than people expect.

**the city** - traffic, crowds, the general chaos of a metro. if you're coming from a smaller city or quieter campus, the adjustment takes time.

**maintaining CG is one of the hardest parts** - grading is relative, competition is high, and you have almost no time for anything outside coursework and project work. extracurriculars become very hard to sustain. be realistic about this.

## 10. what to sort before you even join

**coding (most important):**
- do the striver A-Z sheet if DSA isn't solid
- start LeetCode weekly contests minimum
- Codeforces rounds for speed
- if coding is sorted before sem 1, you're in a much better position

**fundamentals:**
- OS, CN, DBMS, algorithms - revise or learn from scratch if weak
- non-CS background especially - this matters a lot
- probability and linear algebra if planning AI/ML courses

**optional but useful:**
- discrete math and computer architecture if systems is your interest

the idea is simple - if you walk in with coding and fundamentals sorted, you can actually manage sem 1. if you're starting from zero on both while handling coursework and TA duties, it gets very hard very fast.

## so... should you choose IIT Delhi?

**yes, if:**
- placement is a priority and you're willing to do the DSA work
- you want good systems or AI/ML research exposure
- you're okay with 2 years of seriously hard work with no real breaks
- you can handle off-campus life for at least a semester
- you're the kind of person who seeks information and builds connections proactively

**think twice if:**
- you want a relatively relaxed postgrad
- your fundamentals are weak and you're not ready to self-study
- off-campus housing is a genuine financial strain with no buffer
- you're expecting placement to happen to you without consistent effort
- pollution or Delhi's environment is a health dealbreaker

**compared to other IITs:**

iitd placement numbers and faculty quality are strong. the brand matters. if you're comparing with IIT Bombay or IIT Madras - those are genuinely comparable and the right choice depends on specialization, faculty, and what you want to do after. talk to current students at each before deciding.

## 11. some quick things seniors want you to know

a few points straight from current students:

- for JCS, JTM, JRB and similar programs - program core credits are higher, so you get less room to pick electives of your choice. factor this in if course flexibility matters to you.
- MTech students currently can't sit for the on-campus internship season (which starts around Jan) unlike BTechs. this is a gap - industry exposure before placements genuinely helps and hopefully this changes, but for now plan your own internship/project exposure accordingly.
- that said - the current batch has been pushing the department on this and there's likely going to be an arrangement where teams of ~8 students from MTech CSE work on a department-facilitated internship. not confirmed yet but it's being actively worked on - watch this space.
- **on building things vs just studying** - your single most valuable resource here is time. treat it like tokens you can't get back. blogs are fine but code-as-public-good is higher leverage - small apps, tools, things you wish existed. use AI to build faster, no shame in that. 5 years from now saying "i write all my code manually" will be like saying "i make docs without MS Word." things you build improve your skills, your resume, and actually help people. that's the +ev move.
- public goods of any kind - code, guides, tools - are worth doing. just be honest with yourself about the time cost and what you're getting back.
iit delhi is worth it if you go in knowing what you're signing up for and with a real plan. it's not worth it if you're here for the tag and expecting the rest to fall into place.

hope this helped. good luck with the decision.
